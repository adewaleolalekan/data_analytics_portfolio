Choosing the Right Tool for the Job

A case study on selecting the appropriate data analysis tool—Spreadsheets vs. SQL—based on real-world business scenarios.

1

Scenario: Identifying Top 10 Pet Supply Products

The Business Need

A major online pet supplies business needs to identify the top 10 products with the highest sales in the last month. The data is spread across multiple tables in the company's database (one for product details, another for sales data).

Recommended Tool & Justification

Tool: SQL

SQL is the ideal choice here. The data lives in a relational database, and the task requires joining data from multiple tables (products and sales). SQL is designed for exactly this kind of complex query, efficiently handling large datasets and complex relationships far better than a spreadsheet could.

2

Scenario: Determining a New Store Location

The Business Need

A small fitness tech company needs to find which cities have a large customer population to decide on a new retail store location. The data is a single `.csv` file with 300 customer addresses. A simple visualization, like a bar chart, is also required.

Recommended Tool & Justification

Tool: Spreadsheet

A spreadsheet is the perfect tool for this task. The dataset is very small (300 rows) and comes in a simple `.csv` format. Spreadsheets make it easy to quickly sort, filter by city, count customers, and create a simple bar chart for comparison—all without the need for a database setup.

3

Scenario: Analyzing Customer Satisfaction Surveys

The Business Need

A fashion retailer's marketing team has customer satisfaction survey results in a `.csv` file. They need to calculate the average satisfaction score and segment the responses by demographics (age, gender, location) to tailor marketing strategies.

Recommended Tool & Justification

Tool: Spreadsheet

While SQL could be used if the dataset were massive, a spreadsheet is more appropriate here. For a typical survey dataset provided as a `.csv`, a spreadsheet offers powerful and accessible tools like PivotTables to easily calculate average scores and segment data by various demographics without any complex setup.

4

Scenario: Calculating Course Completion Rates

The Business Need

An online education platform needs to identify courses with the lowest completion rates. The data is stored in a relational database with separate tables for user registration, course enrollment, and course completion.

Recommended Tool & Justification

Tool: SQL

This is a classic use case for SQL. The data is in a relational database, and calculating completion rates requires joining information across multiple tables (enrollment vs. completion). SQL can perform these joins and aggregations efficiently across what is likely a very large dataset, making it the superior choice.
